Can Precious Metals Ever Really Go Backward From Here?

By Pranav Gupta Updated May 2026

Sometimes I think about how fast the world changes and honestly it feels crazy.

My nani and dadi used to talk about anna, 1 paisa, 2 paisa and all those things. My mother was born in 1975 and she says even she seen anna disappearing at her time and seen paise used to work at that time. I personally have never even seen anna in real life. Even 25 paise and 50 paise disappeared very quickly after my childhood.

And now look at today.

Sometimes even delivery boys say:
“Bhai rehne do 2 rupay.”

People literally don’t value ₹1 or ₹2 anymore in many places.

Just think about how much changed in only one generation.

People talk about inflation like the world is ending, but if inflation was enough to destroy civilization then why is the world more advanced than ever before?

My nani says gold was around ₹100 per 10 gram around her marriage time. Around my mother’s wedding it was maybe around ₹1000 per 10 gram. Today it is massively higher.

But did civilization collapse in between?

No.

Instead:

  • smartphones came,
  • internet came,
  • AI came,
  • EVs came,
  • digital payments came,
  • technology exploded.

This is why I personally feel human civilization naturally keeps scaling upward.

Every generation thinks:
“This is too expensive.”
“This is impossible.”
“This will never become normal.”

And then suddenly it becomes normal.

There was a time when ₹1 lakh phone sounded insane. Now even vegetable sellers use iPhones sometimes.

There was a time when people could never imagine taxis using ₹40 lakh vehicles. Today people buy expensive cars like Toyota Innova Hycross because reliability matters and they know better products help them earn more money.

Human beings always move toward:

  • speed,
  • comfort,
  • convenience,
  • reliability,
  • and time-saving technology.

Nobody wants to go backward.

People hated phones that took 2 hours to charge and drained twice a day. Companies improved batteries, charging speeds, cooling systems and everything because consumers demanded better technology.

And this is where I started thinking about Silver.

Silver is not just jewelry.

Silver is inside:

  • EVs,
  • solar panels,
  • electronics,
  • AI infrastructure,
  • semiconductors,
  • batteries,
  • power systems.

People say companies will reduce silver usage if silver becomes expensive.

But I think there is a limit to that.

If reducing silver starts reducing performance too much then consumers themselves will reject those products.

Imagine:

  • one EV charges in 15 minutes,
  • another takes 2 hours.

Who will people choose?

Obviously the better technology.

That’s how the world always worked.

People said touchscreen phones are unnecessary once. Then smartphones changed everything and companies like Nokia disappeared from dominance because they didn’t move fast enough with changing technology.

I think the same thing applies to future technologies too.

The world is not trying to go back to old times. Human civilization is trying to become faster, smarter and more advanced every year.

And for that, real-world materials matter.

Unlike Gold which mostly stays locked in banks, lockers and jewelry, silver actually gets consumed in industry.

And yes recycling exists, but nothing recycles 100% forever. Some amount always gets lost.

At the same time technology demand keeps increasing:

  • more AI,
  • more EVs,
  • more electronics,
  • more energy systems,
  • more computing power.

So personally I feel materials like silver could become much more important in the future.

Not because the world is ending.

But because civilization keeps upgrading itself.

Even money itself keeps changing.

Anna disappeared.
Paise disappeared.
₹1 and ₹2 started feeling tiny.
Maybe one day even ₹100 or ₹500 will feel very small normally.

That sounds impossible today.

But honestly so did:

  • AI,
  • smartphones,
  • instant digital payments,
  • and ₹1 lakh phones once.

I feel every 50–100 years humanity enters another massive technology shift.

And maybe we are already living inside one right now.
